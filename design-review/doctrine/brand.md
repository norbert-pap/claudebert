# Brand & Creative Direction

## The Micro-Brand Test — four pillars:

1. **Logo** — Swiss-style. Duotone. Geometric shape connecting to the brand name, or pure logotype. Works as avatar, social icon, signature. If it needs explanation, it's too complex
2. **Typo** — one sans-serif doing double duty for brand and UI. Harmonic type scale. More than one typeface needs justification. The type should feel like a system, not ad-hoc sizes (Bringhurst)
3. **Colors** — 6-8 colors with consistent lightness and contrast. Every color has a named job. Works for brand AND UI
4. **Twist** — a unique visual element for recurring application that differentiates the brand. Recognizable without the logo. The intentional exception to minimalism — fanciness earned by being uniquely *this brand*

## The "Only" Test

(Neumeier): can you say "this is the ONLY ___ that ___"? If the design would look identical with a different logo swapped in, it has no identity.

## Copy Doctrine

- **Lead with outcome** — what the user gets, not what the product does
- **Make it punchy** — short, no filler, every word earns its place
- **Jargon signals belonging** — domain language that makes the target user feel recognized; cut everything else
- **Copy hierarchy is visual hierarchy** — most important thing first, biggest, clearest (Scher)
- **Mine, don't manufacture** — the best copy comes from customer language, not marketer language (Wiebe/Copyhackers)
- **Content design before copywriting** — decide what content is *needed* before writing a word. Often the answer is less content (Richards)

## Product-Grounding Rule

Structural design stays quiet and confident. 1-2 moments where the product itself becomes the visual. Not stock photos — the actual thing. Fanciness earned by relevance to *this specific product*.
