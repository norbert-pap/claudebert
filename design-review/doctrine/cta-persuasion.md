# CTA & Ethical Persuasion — Boag/Click!

Distilled from "Click! How to Encourage Clicks Without Shady Tricks" (Boag, Smashing Magazine). Load during Layer 1 provocations for any site with CTAs, and during Layer 2 for marketing/product sites.

## The Ethics Line

**The journalist test:** if a tactic would embarrass you in a news article, don't use it.

**Dark patterns to flag immediately:**
- Fake urgency/scarcity ("Only 2 left!" when untrue)
- Hidden charges revealed late in funnel
- Confirmshaming ("No thanks, I don't want to save money")
- Forced continuity (silent free-to-paid conversion)
- Roach motel (easy signup, impossible cancellation)
- Misdirection (prominent "accept all" with hidden "reject")

## CTA Design Principles

- **One clear primary CTA per screen.** Secondary CTAs visually de-emphasized. Max 2–3 competing CTAs
- **Embed CTAs in content flow, not sidebars.** Sidebar CTAs live in banner-blindness territory. A content-embedded CTA outperformed a sidebar CTA by 304% in one test
- **Negative space is the CTA's best friend.** Fewer elements near a CTA = more visible
- **Button copy describes what user GETS, not what they DO.** "Get your free report" > "Submit." "Start for free" > "Sign up"
- **Proximity > position.** Where the CTA sits relative to surrounding content matters more than absolute page position
- **"Above the fold" is overrated for CTAs.** Users focus on content; a well-placed CTA below the fold beats a sidebar CTA above the fold

## Directing Attention

- **Faces draw attention biologically.** If a person in a photo looks toward the CTA, it draws attention to it. Looking away = attention away (heatmap-proven)
- **Arrows and directional lines guide the eye** the same way faces do
- **Animation rules:** subtle, 1–2 loops max, user-stoppable, never on secondary content. Looping animations = perceived as ads
- **Color:** CTA button color must be distinct from the rest of the UI palette (not just "make it orange" — it must contrast with the surrounding context)

## Cognitive Load & Simplification

- **Hick's Law:** more choices = slower decisions = higher abandonment. Fewer, clearly distinct options
- **The Attention Points exercise:** users have ~24 attention points (8 seconds × 3 items/sec). Primary CTA needs ≥2 points. Force every element to justify its allocation
- **Three-question test for every element:** (1) What if I removed it? (2) Can I hide it? (3) Can I shrink it?
- **Google vs. Yahoo:** Google concentrates points on search. Yahoo spreads across dozens of elements. Google wins
- **Mood lowers cognitive load.** Friendly illustrations, delightful microcopy, vibrant colors reduce friction (Mailchimp's sweating hand before campaign send)

## Trust Signals (Impact Order)

1. **Third-party validation** — independent reviews (Trustpilot, Google), press, certifications. You can't control these = most credible
2. **Specific social proof** — named customers, measurable outcomes ("cut onboarding by 40%"), video testimonials. Generic praise ("love it!") is worthless
3. **Process transparency** — show how things work. Reduce the black-box feeling
4. **Design quality** — users judge credibility in seconds. Outdated/amateurish design triggers skepticism regardless of product quality
5. **Content quality** — spelling errors, grammar mistakes, low-quality images erode trust

**Trust destroyers:**
- Popups within seconds of arrival (accosting users at the door)
- Stock photos of smiling people in suits (users recognize them as fake)
- Hidden contact information
- Generic stock imagery instead of real team/product photos

## Objection Handling

- Identify top objections from: support logs, sales calls, usability tests, competitor reviews
- Address the most common objections **visibly near the CTA**, not on a separate FAQ page
- **Risk reversal near the CTA:** money-back guarantees, free trials, free returns, no-commitment cancellation
- **Loss framing (ethical):** "You're currently losing $X/month" > "You could save $X/month" — but only if the loss is real

## Perception & Framing

- **Price anchoring:** three-tier pricing works because cheap/expensive tiers frame the middle as best value. Ethical if all options are real
- **WYSIATI (Kahneman):** users decide based only on information visible at the moment. Show the right info at the right time, not all info all the time
- **Representation matters:** images/copy must reflect the target audience. If users don't see people like themselves, they conclude "this isn't for me"
- **Labels must match user mental models, not org chart.** Validate with card sorting. The "Alumni Test": unfamiliar labels don't get ignored — they create confused, misdirected clicks

## Testing CTAs

- **Five-second test:** "What does this site do?" + "Do you trust it?" If users can't answer both, no CTA optimization will help
- **First-click test:** if first click is correct, 87% complete the task. If wrong, only 46%. Tests visual hierarchy directly
- **Semantic differential survey:** rate design on scales (trustworthy↔untrustworthy, modern↔outdated) to validate brand perception
- **Krug method:** 3 users, 1 morning/month, no lab needed. 3 people catch 75% of usability issues
- **End usability sessions with a real offer** — willingness to act reveals persuasiveness, not just usability
